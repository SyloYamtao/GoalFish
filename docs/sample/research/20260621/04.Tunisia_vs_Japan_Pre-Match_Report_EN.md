# 4A. First-Round Group-Stage Match Review

## Tunisia 0-5 (actual 1-5) loss to Sweden · 2026-06-14 · Monterrey BBVA

- Formation and starting XI: Tunisia 3-5-2 — Chamakh; Rekik, Talbi, Ben Hamida; Valery, Khedira, Skhiri (C), Mejbri, Ali Abdi; Saad, Ben Slimane.
- Flow: As early as the 7th minute, Ayari scored with a long-range strike, leaving Tunisia on the back foot; in the 30th minute Isak finished a counterattacking one-on-one (goalkeeper Chamakh spilled the save) to make it 2-0; in the 43rd minute Tunisia pulled one back via a precise Mejbri cross and a Rekik header (1-2 — set pieces/crosses being their only bright spot). In the second half, in the 59th minute Gyökeres pounced on a Skhiri short-passing error in the back to slot home for 3-1, ending the suspense; in the 84th minute substitute Svanberg scored about 18 seconds after coming on (given valid after VAR review) for 4-1; in stoppage time, the 90+6th minute, Ayari added another for 5-1.
- Key events: (1) conceded a long-range goal at kickoff, with the back line offering insufficient protection against shots from outside the box; (2) the goalkeeper's spilled save was a direct gift; (3) captain Skhiri's fatal error handling the ball in his own half led to the third goal — "the man you'd least expect to err, erring."
- Data: shots 13-6, shots on target 7-2, xG 1.36-0.28 (Sweden's efficiency far exceeded their xG, partly thanks to Tunisia's goalkeeping and defensive errors); the two teams' combined first-half xG was only 0.47, and Sweden converted their first two relatively low-quality chances efficiently. Tunisia were not disadvantaged in possession but lacked high-quality chances. Pressing data such as PPDA has no reliable public figures available.
- Real problems exposed: chaotic back-line positioning and marking, insufficient midfield protection of the defense, questionable goalkeeper stability, and a tendency to err when building out under pressure — seriously at odds with the qualifying "clean sheet" image.
- Effective methods: set-piece/cross attacks supplied by Mejbri were the only stable scoring channel (Rekik header).
- Impact on Matchday 2: (1) the starting XI and goalkeeper position are very likely to change; (2) new coach Renard has taken over and may comprehensively rebuild the defensive structure and formation; (3) the heavy defeat brings morale and public-opinion pressure, but also provides a "must respond" mobilizing rationale.
- Small-sample noise note: conceding 5 from only 1.36 xG against contains a clear "opponent overperformance + own individual errors" component, so one should not conclude that Tunisia's long-term defensive level is "concede 5 every match"; however, the string of individual errors is a real signal that warrants high vigilance.

## Japan 2-2 draw with the Netherlands · 2026-06-14 · Arlington (Dallas)

- Formation and starting XI: Japan's three-at-the-back system (recorded by multiple sources as 3-4-2-1, shifting to 5-4-1 when defending) — Suzuki; Taniguchi, Watanabe, H. Ito; Doan, Kamada, Sano, Nakamura; Maeda, Kubo; Ueda (sources differ on individual positions).
- Flow: A cautious first half ended goalless; in the 50th/51st minute Van Dijk met a Gravenberch cross unmarked to head home (0-1); in the 57th minute Kubo laid the ball off and Nakamura's shot from outside the box went in via a deflection off Van Hecke to equalize (1-1); in the 64th minute Summerville cut in from the right and finished with a powerful left-footed strike (1-2); in the 88th/89th minute, from a corner scramble, substitute Koki Ogawa's header flicked off Kamada and went in (effectively a goalkeeper own-goal palm-in), Kamada scoring Japan's latest goal in World Cup history to make it 2-2.
- Substitutions and effects: Moriyasu's second-half substitutions (including bringing on Ogawa) directly created the late equalizer, continuing his "halftime-adjustment" trademark; Kubo was substituted in the 75th minute after a collision with Dumfries (see Section 5).
- Data: possession 40%-60% (ceding possession), shots 10-10, shots on target 3-6, xG 0.59 (Japan) vs 0.79 (Netherlands); the Netherlands' box touches and passing volume (525 passes / 464 completed / 79 final-third entries / 32 box touches) far exceeded Japan's, but they failed to convert into high-quality chances. Japan's fast-transition + compact-low-block strategy worked.
- Key-position performances: goalkeeper Zion Suzuki's commanding and shot-stopping earned praise; Kamada's attacking-midfield linking + late runs were key to the equalizer; Nakamura's counterattacking finishing was outstanding. On the conceding side, they were each beaten once by a cross (Van Dijk header) and a wide cut-in (Summerville) — defensive details that can be targeted.
- Exposed/validated: validated that the "cede possession + counter + resilience" approach works against strong sides and that the team can equalize from behind; exposed that there are still gaps in box defending against crosses and cut-ins.
- Impact on Matchday 2: (1) earned a valuable point, putting them in an active position both psychologically and on points; (2) Kubo's injury is the biggest negative (see Section 5), requiring the attacking organization to be reallocated; (3) physically, after one high-intensity match they head into hot Monterrey — rotation and recovery are variables.
- Small-sample noise note: Japan taking 2 points from only 0.59 xG includes a degree of luck and opponent inefficiency (the Netherlands had many box touches but a relatively low xG); one should not overstate "Japan can comfortably suppress any strong team" — their points came more from strategic choices and in-game resilience.

# 7. Tactical Style Analysis

## Tunisia

- Attacking organization: mainly built on midfield distribution from Skhiri/Khedira and Mejbri's linking and crossing; positional-attack creativity is average, with low xG against strong teams (R1: 0.28).
- Defensive approach: traditionally known for a compact mid-low block + discipline (10 clean sheets in qualifying), but in the first round the low block collapsed against Sweden, with disordered positioning and marking.
- Pressing: primarily a compact defense, not a high-intensity full-pitch pressing type.
- Transition / counter-defense: theoretically has counterattacking DNA, but in the first round was instead cut open by the opponent's counters (Isak one-on-one).
- Wide / central play: attack leans toward wide crosses feeding the center-forward's head (Rekik's goal came from a cross); central penetration is limited.
- Set pieces: on attack, set pieces/crosses are a stable scoring channel (R1's only goal); on defense, there have been historical concerns (e.g., 2018 vs England), but the first-round goals conceded came mainly from long shots and individual errors rather than set pieces.
- Ball progression under pressure: a clear weakness — a Skhiri error in his own half directly led to a goal in R1.
- Breaking down a packed defense: no strong sample yet; overall breakthrough means are limited.

## Japan

- Attacking organization: can both keep and cede possession, strong at fast transitions and counters; Kamada is the organizational hub in the attacking-midfield zone.
- Defensive approach: compact 5-4-1 low block, good collective movement, decisive clearances, and a commanding goalkeeper.
- Pressing: can press when needed and can also actively cede possession to counter — highly flexible.
- Transition / counter-defense: a core strength — in R1, with only 40% possession, they equalized twice purely on transition efficiency.
- Wide / central play: relies on wide pace and cut-in finishing (Nakamura); with Mitoma/Kubo lost, the wide threat declines.
- Set pieces: corner attacks have already produced output (R1's late equalizer came from a corner scramble).
- Ball progression under pressure and breaking down a packed defense: this match may be the first time they face a weaker opponent in a "forced-to-dominate/attacking side" role, so their ability to break down a deep block bears watching (the opposite of R1's "cede possession and counter" scenario — a new test).
